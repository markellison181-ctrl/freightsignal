## ⚖️ Supply/Demand Indicator

**Outbound Tender Rejection Rate (OTRI):** 9.97%

This is the key number. Nearly **1 in 10 loads tendered to carriers is being rejected** — the highest sustained level since the COVID freight boom of 2021–2022. For context:

- 2023 average: 3.2%
- 2024 average: 4.1%
- 2025 average: 5.8%
- **2026 YTD: 8.9%**

**What this means:**
- **For carriers:** You have pricing power you haven't had in 3 years. Reject low-paying freight and reposition into premium lanes.
- **For brokers:** Routing guides are degrading. Expect waterfall rates to kick in more frequently. Build carrier relationships now — transactional sourcing will get expensive fast.
- **For shippers:** Lock in contract rates before the spring produce season. If the Dalilah Law gains traction (see below), capacity will get *much* tighter.

**Carrier Revocations Watch:** FMCSA has revoked or suspended **4,200 operating authorities** YTD in 2026, primarily targeting non-compliant CDL holders under the English Language Proficiency enforcement and non-domiciled CDL restrictions. This is running 340% above the same period in 2025.

## 📰 Trend Story: The Dalilah Law — Could It Create a Trucking Super Cycle?

The biggest story in freight right now isn't tariffs or port congestion — it's a Senate bill that could remove **600,000+ drivers** from U.S. roads.

**The Dalilah Law** (introduced by Sen. Jim Banks, R-Ind., following President Trump's State of the Union address) would:

- Limit CDLs to U.S. citizens, lawful permanent residents, and holders of only E-2, H-2A, and H-2B visas
- Mandate English-only knowledge and skills testing
- Require recertification of all current CDL holders within 180 days
- Withhold federal highway funding from non-compliant states

**The math is brutal.** Foreign-born drivers comprise roughly 18–19% of the U.S. trucking workforce — approximately 630,000–720,000 out of 3.5–3.8 million total CDL holders (BLS). A detailed analysis prepared for J.B. Hunt by transport economist Noël Perry estimates **614,000 drivers** would be disqualified under conservative assumptions:

- ~197,000 from English proficiency failures
- ~252,000 (net) from undocumented status/documentation issues
- ~167,000 (net) from non-domiciled status revocations

**If enacted, the impact would be immediate and severe:**

- Spot rates could surge 50–100% on constrained lanes
- Contract rates would follow within 60–90 days
- Driver wages and sign-on bonuses would spike (potentially $10K–$30K bonuses)
- Small carriers relying on immigrant labor would face existential risk

**The critical difference from past capacity crunches:** During COVID, a wave of new immigrant drivers eventually created excess capacity, contributing to the Great Freight Recession of 2023–2024. The Dalilah Law would permanently close that relief valve.

**Our probability assessment:** The bill has strong White House backing and aligns with broader immigration enforcement. We put passage odds at **35–45%** within 2026, with FMCSA administrative actions achieving 40–50% of the same capacity reduction regardless of legislative outcome.

**Bottom line:** Whether the law passes or not, the directional trend is clear. Capacity is tightening. Spot rates at $2.62/mile are likely the *floor*, not the ceiling, for 2026.

*Sources: FreightWaves, J.B. Hunt/Transport Futures analysis, FMCSA, Bureau of Labor Statistics*

## 📈 Intermodal Watch

Intermodal volumes grew **2% in 2025** (while truckload volumes were negative), building on 8% YoY growth in 2024. The railroads are winning share — but that may be about to change.

If consumer spending surges and inventories continue contracting (ISM data shows faster-than-expected inventory drawdowns in Q4 2025), **urgency will shift freight back to truckload**, where transit times, door-to-door service, and visibility justify the premium.

Watch the intermodal-to-truckload rate spread: currently at **$0.78/mile**, the widest since mid-2022. If truckload rates continue climbing, that spread will narrow and intermodal's volume growth story gets harder.
